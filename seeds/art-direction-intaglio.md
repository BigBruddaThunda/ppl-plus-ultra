# Art Direction — Intaglio Engraving Aesthetic

⚫🟣 — foundational + precise

## One Sentence

The Ppl± visual language is derived from banknote engraving — intaglio line work, guilloche patterns, cross-hatching, and engraved vignettes — colorized for screens and adapted for phone-readable pixel densities, producing an aesthetic that carries authority, craftsmanship, and timelessness without trying to look expensive.

---

## Section 1 — The Style and Its Name

The tradition is called **banknote engraving** or **security printing art**. It is the visual language used on every piece of currency in the world. The techniques are centuries old and have not fundamentally changed because they produce the most information-dense, counterfeit-resistant, universally legible visual artifacts humans have ever designed.

These are the techniques that compose the style:

### Intaglio

The master technique. Lines are incised into a metal plate. Ink fills the grooves. Paper is pressed into the plate under enormous pressure, pulling the ink out. The result: lines with physical depth and tactile texture. On screen, this translates to crisp, deliberate line work with visible weight variation — thick strokes where the burin pressed hard, thin strokes where it glided.

Every element in Ppl± should feel like it was cut into a plate, not drawn on a surface. The lines have intention. Nothing is casual.

### Guilloche

The geometric, lathe-turned patterns that form backgrounds, borders, and security features on currency. Spirograph-like interlocking curves generated by mathematical precision. On currency, these patterns are functional (anti-counterfeiting). In Ppl±, they become:

- Viewport frame borders (each Order has a different guilloche complexity — 🐂 Tuscan is simple, 🏟 Corinthian is ornate)
- Background textures on the stone tabula
- Loading state animations (guilloche patterns drawing themselves)
- Vial ornamentation (the glass container shape uses guilloche curves)
- The Operis header borders

Guilloche patterns are procedurally generated from mathematical parameters. This means the 61 SCL weight dimensions can DRIVE the pattern — different zip codes produce different guilloche borders. The art style becomes computationally derived from the same bus that drives everything else.

### Cross-Hatching and Line Engraving

All shading, depth, and tonal variation is built from lines. No solid fills. No gradients in the traditional sense. Volume comes from:

- **Parallel hatching** — lines running in one direction, density controls darkness
- **Cross-hatching** — two or more sets of parallel lines at angles, density controls tonal depth
- **Stippling** — dots instead of lines for softer tonal areas (used sparingly)

This means every icon, illustration, and decorative element in Ppl± is rendered as line work. The 12 tabula tool icons are line-engraved. The minimap terrain is hatched. The map buildings are architectural line drawings. The vial fluid levels could be rendered as varying line density rather than solid color fills.

### Engraved Vignettes

The portrait and scene illustrations on banknotes. Photorealistic or near-photorealistic imagery rendered entirely in lines — no washes, no fills, no photographic reproduction. Just thousands of precisely placed lines that, viewed at normal distance, resolve into images with full tonal range.

In Ppl±, vignettes appear in:
- Cosmogram illustrations (deep research documents get engraved scene headers)
- Operis edition headers (each day's edition has a vignette tied to the Order and season)
- Deck identity illustrations (each of the 42 decks gets a characteristic vignette)
- Exercise library entries (movement illustrations in engraved style)
- The viewport default state (when no content is loaded, the viewport shows an engraved scene of the current zip code's character)

### Copperplate / Steel Engraving Typography

Currency uses engraved lettering — not typeset fonts but hand-cut letterforms with characteristic serif weight, consistent stroke width, and mathematical spacing. In Ppl±, this informs:

- Headline typography for the Operis, deck names, and zip code labels
- The viewport frame corner labels (zip code, floor indicator)
- The stone tabula icon labels
- Any text that appears "engraved into" a surface rather than "printed on" it

## Section 2 — Why This Style

### Authority Without Arrogance

Currency art carries authority because it represents the full faith of institutions. But it doesn't try to impress — it tries to be trusted. A dollar bill doesn't have a marketing team. It has a printing bureau. The aesthetic communicates: this is real, this was made carefully, this means something.

Ppl± needs the same register. Not luxury. Not minimalism. Not tech-startup clean. Craftsmanship that communicates: someone built this with care, and it will still be here tomorrow.

### Timelessness

The intaglio style has been in continuous use since the 15th century. The basic visual grammar of a banknote from 1850 is readable today. The basic visual grammar of a banknote from 2025 would be readable in 1850. No other visual style in human history has this kind of temporal durability.

For a project architected on a 100-year timeline, this is not decoration. It is structural. The visual language should age as well as the architecture.

### Universality

Every culture on Earth uses currency. Every human recognizes the visual language of engraved line work, guilloche borders, and vignette illustrations. The style transcends language, culture, and era. This matches Ppl±'s universal accessibility goal — the visual language works for anyone, anywhere.

### Different From Everything Else

Every fitness app uses: flat design, gradient backgrounds, bold sans-serif type, photography-heavy layouts, neon accent colors, rounded corners, card-based UI with drop shadows.

Ppl± using intaglio engraving is immediately, unmistakably different. Not different for its own sake — different because the style matches the system's character. The style IS the system. An architectural training language rendered in an architectural visual language.

### The Grandfather's Gift

The style reinforces the grandfather's gift principle (see `seeds/life-copilot-architecture.md`). Intaglio engraving is the style of almanacs, certificates, diplomas, savings bonds, maps, and technical illustrations. It is the visual language of things your grandfather would hand you and say "keep this, it's worth something." Not flashy. Not trendy. Worth something.

## Section 3 — Colorization

Traditional banknote engraving uses limited color — typically 2-4 ink colors per side. Ppl± uses the full 8-Color palette. The colorization rules:

### Color as Ink Layer

Each SCL Color maps to a literal ink color in the engraving. When content is tagged with a Color, the engraved elements use that Color as the primary ink:

- ⚫ Teaching — dark charcoal/black ink. The most traditional engraving register.
- 🟢 Bodyweight — forest green ink. Like the reverse of a US dollar.
- 🔵 Structured — deep blue ink. Like a UK banknote or architectural blueprint.
- 🟣 Technical — purple/violet ink. Like a vintage revenue stamp.
- 🔴 Intense — deep red ink. Like a stock certificate or urgent notice.
- 🟠 Circuit — warm amber/orange ink. Like a lithographic poster.
- 🟡 Fun — golden/ochre ink. Like a vintage map or treasure chart.
- ⚪ Mindful — silver/light gray ink. Like a pencil preliminary drawing.

### Multi-Color Engraving

When multiple Colors are active (vials illuminated, mixed-Color content), the engraving uses multiple ink layers — like a multi-pass print where each Color contributes its line work. The overlapping lines create the visual complexity of a real banknote's multi-color security printing.

### Background and Paper

The "paper" texture varies by Order (the D-module design proportions from `design-system.ts`):

- 🐂 Foundation — rough, warm parchment. Visible fiber texture.
- ⛽ Strength — heavy cotton stock. Clean, dense.
- 🦋 Hypertrophy — smooth, slightly glossy. Like a new banknote.
- 🏟 Performance — crisp white. Clinical precision.
- 🌾 Full Body — natural, unbleached. Organic grain.
- ⚖ Balance — watermarked appearance. Subtle pattern in the paper itself.
- 🖼 Restoration — aged, softened. Like a well-handled letter.

## Section 4 — Phone Readability at Small Pixels

The challenge: intaglio's power is in fine line detail. Phone screens have limited resolution. The solution is a **resolution cascade** — the style simplifies as the render size decreases, maintaining the FEELING of engraving at every scale.

### Resolution Levels

**Full resolution (viewport content, desktop):**
Full cross-hatching, detailed guilloche, readable vignettes. The closest to actual banknote fidelity. Line weight varies. Multiple hatching angles. Fine detail rewards close inspection.

**Medium resolution (viewport on phone, tabula icons at tablet size):**
Simplified hatching (fewer line sets). Guilloche patterns reduce to simpler curves. Vignettes become more iconic, less photorealistic. Line weight increases slightly for screen legibility. Still unmistakably engraved.

**Small resolution (minimap, vial ornamentation, tabula icons on phone):**
Hatching reduces to simple directional lines or stipple dots. Guilloche becomes a single-line border pattern. Icons are recognizable silhouettes with minimal engraved detail — like the simplified engravings on coins rather than bills. The style is present but compressed.

**Pixel scale (fog of war map elements, notification badges, tiny indicators):**
The engraving style reduces to its most essential gesture: line weight variation and deliberate stroke placement. Even a 16×16 pixel icon can carry the intaglio feeling if the lines are placed with intention rather than using standard vector icon conventions.

### The Pixel Art Analytique Connection

The central map's pixel art analytique style (from the home screen seed) IS the intaglio style at pixel resolution. The NES Hyrule map aesthetic is what intaglio engraving looks like when compressed to low-resolution grid rendering. The map isn't a different style from the rest of the app — it's the same style at a different zoom level. Zoom into the map and the pixel blocks resolve into hatched line work. Zoom out and the hatching compresses into pixel blocks.

This is fractal consistency. The style is the same at every scale. Only the resolution changes.

## Section 5 — Procedural Generation from Weight Vectors

The intaglio style is not just decorative — it is computationally derivable from the SCL weight system. This is the architectural bridge between the art direction and the middle-math engine.

### Weight Vector → Line Parameters

The 61-dimensional weight vector for each zip code can drive engraving parameters:

| Weight Dimension | Visual Parameter |
|-----------------|------------------|
| Order weight (load ceiling) | Line weight / stroke thickness. Higher Order = heavier lines. |
| Axis weight (exercise character) | Hatching angle. Each Axis biases the line direction. |
| Type weight (muscle groups) | Line density / hatching frequency. More targeted = denser hatching. |
| Color weight (equipment/format) | Ink color and saturation. Direct Color mapping. |
| Block weights | Compositional zones within the viewport frame. |
| Operator weight | Guilloche complexity. More expressive operators = more ornate borders. |

### Procedural Guilloche from Zip Code

A guilloche pattern is defined by a small set of mathematical parameters (frequency, amplitude, phase, number of passes). The 4-digit zip code can seed these parameters:

```
Order (1-7)  → base frequency
Axis (1-6)   → amplitude multiplier
Type (1-5)   → phase offset
Color (1-8)  → number of overlapping curves
```

Each zip code produces a unique guilloche pattern. 1,680 zip codes = 1,680 distinct border patterns. The pattern IS the zip code rendered as geometry.

### Procedural Vignette Generation

Future: AI image generation constrained to intaglio line style could produce unique vignettes for each of the 42 decks, the 1,680 zip codes, or even the 2,085 exercises. The constraint is: output must look like it was engraved, not photographed or digitally illustrated. Line work only. Hatching for tone. No fills.

## Section 6 — Application Across the System

### The Relevator

- **Minimap:** terrain rendered in simple hatching. Paths as single engraved lines. Buildings as solid outlines with minimal internal hatching. Water features as wave-pattern lines.
- **Stone tabula:** the surface texture IS a guilloche pattern. Tool icons are engraved into the stone. Active icons have ink in their grooves (colored). Inactive icons are unfilled grooves (gray relief).
- **Vials:** glass containers with guilloche-patterned glass. Fluid rendered as horizontal line density (more lines = more fluid). Sludge as darker, thicker, irregular hatching at the bottom.
- **Axis tabs:** each tab has a small engraved glyph of its Axis symbol. Active tab is inked. Inactive tabs are relief-only.

### The Terminal

- Text is clean and legible — the terminal zone is the one area where the engraving style is minimal. Text readability trumps ornamentation.
- Channel tabs have subtle guilloche borders.
- Hyperlink text uses the Color-as-ink principle (links are colored by destination).

### The Viewport Frame

- The frame border IS a guilloche pattern driven by the current zip code's weight vector.
- Corner ornaments in the engraved vignette style. Each corner shows one of the 4 dial values as an engraved emblem.
- The frame changes subtly with each dial spin — the guilloche parameters shift.

### The Operis

- Edition headers use full engraved vignette illustrations.
- Body text is clean, but pull quotes and section dividers use engraved line ornaments.
- The day's Color-of-the-Day determines the ink color of the edition's decorative elements.

### Workout Cards

- Block headers (♨️ Warm-Up, 🧈 Bread & Butter, etc.) have engraved border separators.
- Exercise lines use the ± superscript/subscript boxes rendered as engraved data cells.
- The card itself, when viewed in the viewport, has a banknote-like composition: border, header vignette, structured data in the center, footer with closing information.

### Cosmograms and Deep Content

- This is where the style reaches full expression. Cosmograms are the illuminated manuscripts of the system.
- Full vignette illustrations. Dense cross-hatching. Ornate guilloche borders. Multi-color ink layers.
- These are the pages that reward zoom-in. The detail is there for those who look.

### Loading States

- Instead of spinners or progress bars: guilloche patterns drawing themselves in real-time.
- A loading state is a guilloche animation — curves tracing their mathematical paths. The pattern completes when the content is ready.
- This turns loading from dead time into visual engagement. Each loading animation is driven by the destination zip code's parameters — you see the room's signature pattern before the room itself loads.

## Section 7 — What This Is Not

- **Not skeuomorphic.** The goal is not to make the phone screen look like a banknote. The goal is to use the visual techniques of banknote engraving as a design language. No fake paper textures, no trompe l'oeil depth effects, no "realistic currency" gimmicks.
- **Not maximalist.** The engraving style is used with restraint. Most of the viewport is clean content. The engraving appears in borders, frames, icons, ornaments, and illustrations — not on every pixel.
- **Not retro for retro's sake.** The style is timeless, not vintage. It should feel current and alive, not like a historical reproduction.
- **Not expensive-looking.** This is the style of public institutions (treasury departments, postal services, national parks), not luxury brands. It communicates civic care, not wealth.
- **Not hand-drawn.** The line work is precise and mathematical. It feels engraved by a master craftsman using precision tools, not sketched freehand. Deliberate, not organic.

## Section 8 — Implementation Path

### Phase 1: Design Tokens (existing)

The 8 Color palettes and 7 Order D-module proportions in `design-tokens.json` already encode the parameters that drive colorization and compositional density. These become the ink colors and paper textures.

### Phase 2: Border and Frame System

Build a guilloche generator that takes zip code parameters and outputs SVG border patterns. Apply to viewport frame, card borders, and section dividers. This is the first visible expression of the art direction.

### Phase 3: Icon System

Re-render all tabula tool icons and Axis tab glyphs in the intaglio line style. 12 tool icons + 6 Axis glyphs + 8 Color vial ornaments + system icons (back, settings, notifications).

### Phase 4: Map Rendering

The figure-ground map in the viewport uses the intaglio style at pixel resolution. Buildings as outlined engraved forms. Terrain as hatched zones. Paths as single-weight lines. Fog of war as absence of engraving (blank paper).

### Phase 5: Vignette Library

Build or generate engraved vignette illustrations for the 42 decks, the 7 Orders, the 12 operators, and the Operis header templates. This is the content-rich visual layer.

### Phase 6: Procedural Pipeline

Connect the weight vector system to the guilloche generator and hatching parameters so that every zip code's visual rendering is derived from its mathematical identity. The art direction becomes automated — same as how workout content is derived from the SCL.

## Open Questions

1. **Guilloche generation library.** What tooling generates SVG guilloche patterns from parameters? Custom? Existing library? D3.js-based?
2. **Line weight at 1x phone resolution.** What's the minimum stroke width that reads on a 375px screen? Testing needed.
3. **Performance.** Complex SVG line patterns may impact rendering performance on mobile. Canvas vs. SVG vs. pre-rendered raster tradeoffs.
4. **Accessibility.** Does the hatching style work with screen readers and high-contrast modes? The line work needs to degrade gracefully.
5. **Vignette sourcing.** AI-generated? Commissioned from engraving artists? Hybrid (AI draft, human refinement)?
6. **Animation.** How do guilloche loading animations perform on low-end devices? Fallback to simpler patterns?
7. **Dark mode.** How does the engraving style adapt? Inverted ink (light lines on dark ground)? Like a chalkboard engraving? Or maintain traditional ink-on-paper?
8. **Print.** If users export workout cards or Operis editions, the intaglio style should print beautifully on paper. The style literally comes from print. This is a feature, not a constraint.
